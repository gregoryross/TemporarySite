Research

My goal is to improve the technology behind computer aided drug design,
and to develop methods that help medicinal chemists decide
what sort of compounds to consider when synthesizing drug candidates.

Atomistic simulations for drug discovery

Much of my research resolves around atomistic simulations of biomolecules, by which I mean proteins, drugs, solvent (e.g. water) and other small molecules that are relevant for molecular biology. The simulations use models of the forces between atoms to quantitatively predict things that those in drug discovery care about, such as the binding affinities between of a set of drug candidates and a protein that is implicated in a disease. Being atomistic in resolution, the simulations give us an unprecedented level of detail on the systems we study. So, in addition to using simulations for quantitative predictions, we can use them to generate hypotheses to address questions like ‘how does this particular drug bind?’ and ‘how does this particular protein function?’.

Despite being very simplified models of the atomistic world, the simulations are very complex and time intensive. For a typically sized protein immersed in water, the simulations have to evaluate the forces between tens of thousands of atoms over hundreds of thousands of iterations. Depending on the system and the sort of questions one is trying to answer, simulations can take days to months. Despite the increasing power of computer processors and smarter use of hardware, sampling the important states of the biomolecules still remains a challenge. Broadly speaking, the two main problems my field grapples with are to address are to 1) improve the accuracy of the models of the forces, and 2) improve the thoroughness of the sampling. A lot of my research has been on the latter.

I am also very keen on reducing the barriers to using atomistic simulations. They require expertise to set-up, run, and analyse. One large barrier is the complexity of the software we use, a problem that is compounded in academic research as software is frequently augmented additional functionality. This in turn raises issues of the sustainability of the software itself. I strive to make all my software easy to use, free, open-source, and written in an interpretable way.
