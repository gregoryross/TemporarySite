Research

My goal is to improve the technology behind computer aided drug design,
and to develop methods that help medicinal chemists decide
what sort of compounds to consider when synthesizing drug candidates.

Atomistic simulations for drug discovery

Much of my research revolves around atomistic simulations of biomolecules, and by 'biomolecules', I mean proteins, drugs, solvent (e.g. water) and other small molecules that are of biological interest. The simulations model the forces between atoms to predict configurations and dynamical processes, which in turn can be used to estimate quantities can that verified (or falsified) experimentally. Being atomistic in resolution, the simulations give us an unprecedented level of detail on the systems in question, so we can use them to generate hypotheses to address questions like ‘how does this particular drug bind?’ and ‘how does this particular protein function?’.

Despite being very simplified models of the atomistic world, the simulations are very complex and time intensive. For a typically sized protein immersed in water, the simulations have to evaluate the forces between tens of thousands of atoms over hundreds of thousands of iterations. Depending on the system and the sort of questions one is trying to answer, simulations can take days to months. Despite the increasing power of computer processors and smarter use of hardware, sampling the important states of the biomolecules still remains a challenge. Broadly speaking, the two main problems my field grapples with are 1) improving the accuracy of the modelled atomistic forces, and 2) improving the thoroughness of the sampling. A lot of my research has been on the latter, particular with enhancing the sampling of water in buried protein cavities (for instance, see this paper).

I am also very keen on reducing the barriers to using atomistic simulations. They require expertise to set-up, run, and analyse. One large barrier is the complexity of the software we use, a problem that is compounded in academic research due to the frequent augmentations and modifications that are made to the software as the science progresses. This in turn raises issues of the sustainability of the software itself. I strive to make all my software easy to use, free, open-source, and written in an interpretable way. I'm currently a developer for ProtoMS and work on tools for OpenMM. Another barrier to using atomistic simulations is knowing how make reliable inferences from the large and complex data that is produced. It is important to ask the the right questions from the data, and have the best tools to address them. As a result, I'm really interested in statistics (with a Bayesian flavour) and machine learning.
